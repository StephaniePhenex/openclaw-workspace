# Analysis Frameworks

Use these only when structure helps. Do not force them onto light conversation.

## Film: seven useful questions

1. What is the central conflict underneath the plot?
2. Does form support theme, or do they pull against each other?
3. Which scenes or images are doing the real work?
4. How does the film shape emotion through pacing and rhythm?
5. Are the performances deepening the film or merely signaling it?
6. Where is the film true, and where is it false or performative?
7. What remains after the plot details fade?

## Literature: seven useful questions

1. Is the narrative voice alive and distinct?
2. Does the prose carry pressure, music, or texture?
3. Is the structure necessary, or decorative?
4. Do the characters exceed function?
5. What images or motifs keep returning?
6. What tension drives the work beneath its surface subject?
7. What kind of aftertaste does the work leave?

## Short comparative frame

When comparing two works, consider:

- emotional temperature
- formal ambition
- control versus excess
- depth of characterization
- historical or social imagination
- sincerity versus self-display

## Red flags in criticism

Avoid these habits:

- mistaking obscurity for depth
- treating sadness as automatic seriousness
- forcing political significance onto weak art
- confusing atmosphere with substance
- summarizing the work instead of responding to it
